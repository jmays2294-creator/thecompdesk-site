How the Point System Works

Under the 2012 NYS Impairment Guidelines, radiculopathy — nerve root compression causing radiating pain, sensory loss, or motor deficits — is evaluated using a point-based scoring system across four clinical categories defined in Sections S11.4 through S11.7. The total points determine the Schedule Loss of Use (SLU) percentage assigned to the affected extremity.

§S11.4 — Sensory Loss / Pain (0–3 pts)

Scores the degree of sensory deficit in the affected dermatomal distribution, from no loss to severe/constant dysesthesias.

§S11.5 — Loss of Power (0–3 pts)

Scores motor deficit by manual muscle testing grade in the affected myotome. 5/5 = 0 pts, down to 2/5 or less = 3 pts.

§S11.6 — Loss of Reflexes (0–2 pts)

Scores deep tendon reflex changes at the affected level. Normal = 0 pts, diminished = 1 pt, absent = 2 pts.

§S11.7 — Electrodiagnostic (0–3 pts)

Scores EMG/NCV findings from normal to severe denervation with absent motor units or significant conduction block.

The affected nerve root determines which extremity is evaluated: cervical roots (C4–C8) are assessed as loss of use of the arm (312 weeks maximum under WCL §15(3)), while lumbar/sacral roots (L3–S1) are assessed as loss of use of the leg (288 weeks maximum).

Actual SLU awards depend on the totality of medical evidence, the treating physician's and IME physician's opinions, and the Workers' Compensation Board judge's determination. The point system provides a structured framework for evaluating impairment, but the Board retains discretion in all findings.
