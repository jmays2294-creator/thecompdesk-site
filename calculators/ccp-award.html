About Classified Awards in New York

Classified awards are formal determinations by the Workers' Compensation Board that establish ongoing benefits for permanently disabled workers. The three main types of classifications in New York are Permanent Partial Disability (PPD), Permanent Total Disability (PTD), and Death Benefits. Each classification type has different requirements and benefit structures.

Permanent Partial Disability (PPD): PPD classifications are issued when a worker has reached maximum medical improvement and has sustained a permanent injury that reduces wage earning capacity, but can still work in some capacity. The PPD award typically consists of a weekly benefit based on a percentage of loss of wage earning capacity, multiplied by the compensation rate, and continues until the worker reaches age 65 or returns to regular employment. The award may be calculated based on Loss of Wage Earning Capacity (LWEC) or as a lump sum settlement.

Permanent Total Disability (PTD): A PTD classification is issued when the worker is permanently and completely unable to work due to the work injury. PTD benefits continue for life or until the worker returns to work, and provide a full weekly benefit equal to the worker's compensation rate. PTD is a permanent classification that provides lifetime benefits.

Death Benefits: When a worker dies as a result of a work-related injury, the worker's dependents may be entitled to death benefits. These include funeral expenses (up to a statutory maximum) and weekly benefits for eligible dependents, such as spouses and children. Death benefit calculations depend on the number of eligible dependents and may also include vocational rehabilitation for any surviving children.
